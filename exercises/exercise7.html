Navigating Duality: Marrant's Journey with Identity and Faith

An evaluation of the life of John Marrant, an 18th century preacher and author whose dramatic depiction of his religious journey reflected the desperation of some Black Americans to appeal to white American audiences. Marrant's perception of his own identity reveals uncomfortable truths about the effects of faith on marginalized peoples. When read through the lens of binary opposition, Marrant's contorted view of his religion conveys his fear of the white supremacist powers of 18th century America.

Time as a Parable in Sonny's Blues

An analysis of James Baldwin's Sonny's Blues, an evocative short story following two brothers in mid-1900s Harlem. This essay explores the role of time as a transformative force in Sonny's Blues, analyzing how James Baldwin uses temporal fluidity to illuminate identity, relationships, and generational trauma. Through Sonny's climactic performance, the essay examines the collapse of time, the interplay between music and emotional reconciliation, and the power of art as a means of survival and redemption. By connecting personal suffering to collective history, the essay highlights Baldwin's profound commentary on the human condition, emphasizing the resilience and healing potential found in artistic expression.
